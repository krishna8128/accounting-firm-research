# ICAI / Regulatory Environment for a CA-Tooling SaaS — A Rules-Based Brief

**Research date:** 16 May 2026
**Scope:** ICAI Code of Ethics (12th edition currently in force; 13th edition effective 1 Apr 2026), Chartered Accountants Act 1949, NFRA orders 2024-26, DPDPA 2023 + DPDP Rules 2025, MDP draft consultation (Sep 2025), ICAI CMP listing programme.
**Audience:** Founder of a bootstrapped B2B SaaS for Indian CA firms.
**Posture:** Conservative rules-of-the-road. Where a rule binds the *customer* (the CA), it indirectly binds the *vendor* through what customers can accept.

---

## 0. Executive Summary

Three rules will most aggressively shape a CA-tooling SaaS business model. None of them are catastrophic. All three need to be designed *around*, not *through*.

1. **Clause (6) — Anti-Solicitation** in Part I of the First Schedule, CA Act 1949 — your customers may not solicit. You cannot use them to solicit either. No testimonials with name, no client-logo carousel, no "What top CAs say about us" marketing pages without serious risk to the very customer you are showcasing.
2. **Clause (2) — Fee-Sharing with Non-Members** — your CA customers cannot share fees, commissions, or brokerage with a non-CA SaaS company. Subscription SaaS pricing is **clean**. Success-fee / revenue-share pricing is **structurally blocked** at the customer side.
3. **NFRA's SA 230 audit-file discipline (Circular NF-22/52/2025-NFRA, 16 Dec 2025)** — for audits of Public Interest Entities (PIEs), audit files must be assembled within 60 days, archived for 7+ years, in their original electronic form, and producible to NFRA within 7 days of request. Any SaaS handling audit working-papers is implicitly subject to these data-handling expectations.

The DPDPA 2023, with Rules notified 13 Nov 2025, layers on top with an 18-month transition (most substantive obligations bite ~May 2027), penalties up to ₹250 cr, and 72-hour breach notification. The MDP draft (MCA consultation closed 30 Sep 2025) is real but slow — implementation by mid-2026 at the earliest, more likely 12-18 months out.

## 1. ICAI Code of Ethics — Advertising, Solicitation, and Marketing a SaaS to CAs

### 1.1 The governing text

The First Schedule, Part I of the **Chartered Accountants Act, 1949** is the statutory source. The **ICAI Code of Ethics (12th edition, applicable from 1 July 2020)** elaborates. The **13th edition (released as exposure draft 27 Oct 2025, effective 1 Apr 2026)** modernises but does *not* lift the core prohibitions.

The two binding clauses for SaaS marketing are:

**Clause (6) — Solicitation.** "A chartered accountant in practice shall be deemed to be guilty of professional misconduct, if he solicits clients or professional work either directly or indirectly by circular, advertisement, personal communication or interview or by any other means."

**Clause (7) — Advertising Attainments.** Prohibits advertising professional attainments or services, or using any designation or expressions other than chartered accountant on professional documents, visiting cards, letter heads or sign boards, unless it be a degree of a University established by law in India or recognised by the Central Government or a title indicating membership of ICAI.

These bind the *CA*, not the SaaS vendor directly. But they make it professional misconduct for a CA to *participate* in vendor marketing that constitutes solicitation.

### 1.2 What a SaaS can and cannot legally publish about CA customers

| Marketing artefact | Legal? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Anonymised case study ("a 12-partner firm in Mumbai reduced GST recon time by 40%") | **Yes** | No identifying info, no solicitation by CA |
| Case study with firm name and partner photo | **No** | The CA is being held out as endorser → indirect solicitation under Clause (6); ICAI 13th edition explicitly continues to ban testimonials |
| Customer logo wall on the SaaS website | **No, in practice** | "Cannot disclose client names or display their logos anywhere to showcase your portfolio… constitutes indirect solicitation." This bites the CA, not you. CA firms will refuse to consent or quietly pull consent the moment ICAI Ethics Committee writes. |
| Video testimonial: "CA Sharma from XYZ & Co. says…" | **No** | Direct violation. Testimonials are explicitly classified as laudatory promotion. |
| "Best CA tool" / "ranked #1 by CAs" / star ratings | **No** | The ICAI Code disallows laudatory or comparative claims tied to CAs. (Bites your customers if they republish the claim.) |
| A CA writing a paid review of your tool on her blog | **No** | The CA would violate Clause (6). She can write an unpaid technical opinion, but the moment money or in-kind consideration flows, it is professional misconduct. |
| Big-4 logos in marketing ("trusted by Big 4") | **No, do not** | The Big 4 Indian affiliates aggressively protect logos and audit-firm policies prohibit endorsement; aside from copyright/trademark, you risk corporate-litigation exposure. |
| "ICAI" name or logo on your website | **Absolutely not** | ICAI is a registered trademark (registration #2121118 for "ICAI"; #2147610 for the CA logo). Unauthorised use violates Section 24A of the Chartered Accountants Act 1949 and the Trade Marks Act 1999. ICAI has sued a shoe company for ₹1 crore over CA-logo misuse. The only exception is ICAI's own *empanelment* — see CMP listing in §6. |
| "Listed on ICAI CMP" (if actually listed) | **Yes**, with the official empanelment usage rules | If you have the empanelment, you can identify the empanelment factually; you cannot imply ICAI *endorses* your product. |

### 1.3 What changes under the 13th edition (1 Apr 2026)

The 13th edition relaxes some restrictions for *CA firms themselves*:

- Firms may use **push technology** (e.g., LinkedIn posts, email newsletters) for **non-exclusive services** like accounting and consultancy — but still not for audit/attest, where Clause (6) remains absolute.
- Removal of font-size limits and the mandatory membership-number/FRN display on letterheads.
- "Contemporary form" advertisements (write-ups, directories, online write-ups) explicitly permitted.
- **Testimonials, paid Google/Facebook ads, client-logo display, and laudatory language remain prohibited.**

For a SaaS vendor, the practical consequence is that *your customers* can do more outreach in 2026 — which actually expands the partnership/co-marketing surface area for vendors who structure things carefully (e.g., your CA customer can attend a webinar you host; they cannot endorse your product on stage).

### 1.4 The "CA who endorses your product" trap

A CA who endorses your SaaS publicly (LinkedIn post saying "I use Suvit, it's brilliant, sign up here") risks Clause (6) action if (a) there is any consideration (cash, equity, free seats, conference travel) or (b) the endorsement carries a referral link. Without consideration *and* without referral attribution, it is generally treated as personal opinion and tolerated. **Founder rule:** Do not pay influencer CAs without an indemnity. Do not give a CA equity if you intend to use them for marketing. Get every endorsement in writing as "personal technical opinion" if you must use it.

### 1.5 Practical marketing playbook (legal)

- Anonymised case studies ("Tier-2 firm, 8 partners, Pune").
- Aggregated benchmarks ("avg. 38% time saved, n=42 CA firms").
- Thought-leadership co-authored with named CAs **without product endorsement**.
- Webinars and roundtables (CA speakers permitted; product pitch by SaaS founder).
- Self-published demos and technical content.
- Empanelment on ICAI CMP (legitimate badge).
- Independent CA student/community awards (e.g., ICAI's own innovation awards), if won.

## 2. Fee-Sharing with Non-CAs — What This Means for SaaS Pricing

### 2.1 The governing text

**Clause (2), Part I, First Schedule, CA Act 1949:** "A chartered accountant in practice shall be deemed to be guilty of professional misconduct, if he pays or allows any share, commission or brokerage in the fees or profits of his professional business, to any person other than a member of the Institute or a partner or a retired partner or the legal representative of the deceased partner or a member of any other professional body or with such other persons having such qualifications as may be prescribed."

**Clause (3):** prohibits the inverse — *accepting* profits from non-members' work.

**Clause (5):** prohibits securing professional business through "non-permitted means" (intermediaries who aren't partners/employees).

**Clause (10):** prohibits charging fees based on a *percentage of profits* (with carve-outs: receivership, co-op audits, valuations under specific Rules).

### 2.2 SaaS pricing models — clean vs. blocked

| Pricing model | Legal? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Flat subscription** (₹X/month per seat, ₹Y/firm/year) | **Clean** | No share in CA's fee revenue. This is the default and what 99% of CA-tooling SaaS use. |
| **Per-deliverable / per-transaction** (₹X per return filed, ₹Y per invoice processed) | **Clean** | Vendor charges for usage; not a share of CA's fee. |
| **Tiered usage-based** (₹X/100 GSTRs, ₹Y/1000 ledger lines) | **Clean** | Same as above. |
| **Revenue share** ("we take 10% of your fee from each client you onboard via us") | **BLOCKED** | Direct violation of Clause (2) for the CA. The CA cannot legally pay this. |
| **Success fee on GST refund** ("we take 15% of refund recovered") | **BLOCKED for CAs** to charge | Clause (10) blocks the CA from charging a % of recovery (with narrow exceptions). If the SaaS itself contracts directly with the *taxpayer* for the refund work, this is non-CA work and Clause (10) does not bite — but then the SaaS is itself doing a regulated service and faces separate problems. |
| **Success fee on tax saved** | **BLOCKED for CAs** | Same logic as above. |
| **Referral commission to CAs** ("refer 3 clients to our SaaS, get ₹X") | **BLOCKED via Clause (5) and Clause (6)** | The CA cannot solicit. Receiving a referral commission from a non-CA is fee-sharing in reverse and falls under Clause (3) as well. |
| **Co-branded service** where SaaS takes % of CA's fees on co-delivered engagements | **BLOCKED** | Same as revenue share. |
| **Equity in a CA firm** (SaaS company holds equity stake in CA practice) | **BLOCKED until MDP rules change** | A CA firm cannot have non-CA partners except as allowed by MDP framework (six recognised professions; IT/SaaS not yet on the list). |
| **Software-as-a-service with bundled compliance work** | **GREY** | If your SaaS bundles human review by your in-house CA, you need an MDP-compliant structure or strict separation of the SaaS subscription fee from any "CA-rendered services" line. |

### 2.3 Net guidance

Build the pricing on **subscription + per-deliverable usage**. Anything that looks like a percentage of the CA's professional fee will eventually hit Disciplinary Directorate scrutiny — the customer takes the regulatory hit, not you, but your business model dies because no CA will sign.

Success-fee pricing is viable **only** if the SaaS contracts directly with the *taxpayer/end-client* (not with the CA), positions the work as non-regulated tax advisory done by the SaaS's own non-CA team, and uses CA labour only behind the scenes via white-label or employment arrangements. This is the **Vakilsearch / IndiaFilings model** — and it courts ICAI scrutiny for "doing what is reserved for CAs."

## 3. Multi-Disciplinary Partnership (MDP) — Where the Rules Are Heading

### 3.1 Current state (as of May 2026)

**The 2021 ICAI MDP framework** (Regulation 53B) already allows a CA to partner with members of six specified professional bodies: Company Secretary (ICSI), Cost Accountant (ICMAI), Advocate (Bar Council), Architect, Engineer (IEI), and Actuary. **For statutory audit, majority of partners must be CAs by both number and profit share.**

What's **not** allowed today: MBAs, valuers (RVO members are partial exception), insolvency professionals (unless also CA/CS/CMA), IT professionals, technology entrepreneurs. This is the most important constraint for a SaaS startup considering equity-style integration with a CA firm — *you cannot today take a partnership interest in a CA firm if you're not one of the six recognised professionals.*

### 3.2 The Sep 2025 MCA consultation

On **17 September 2025**, the Ministry of Corporate Affairs published a background note inviting public comments on establishment of Indian Multi-Disciplinary Partnership firms; deadline closed **30 September 2025**. The note is broader than the 2021 ICAI framework and contemplates partnerships that span legal, accounting, valuation, technology, and advisory disciplines under an LLP structure, with:

- **Minimum 10-year experience thresholds** for partners
- **Independent oversight boards**
- **Mandatory rotation** for key positions
- **Technology-driven audit trails** for compliance
- **Ethical firewalls** for conflicts of interest

The political motivation is the "**Desi Big 4**" project — FM Sitharaman's May 2024 ask to ICAI to create an Indian firm at Big 4 scale, part of the 100-day Viksit Bharat 2047 agenda; reiterates PM Modi's 2017 call. The structural problem the policy is trying to solve: of 95,000 ICAI-registered firms, ~70,000 are proprietorships; only ~400 have more than 10 partners. Big 4 affiliates dominate 67% of Nifty-500 engagements; ~$10-15 bn/year capital outflow to overseas consultants.

### 3.3 What MDP enables for a SaaS startup

If the expanded MDP rules pass, **technology professionals could become partners** in a CA firm. This unlocks three new business motions:

1. **Equity in CA firms.** A SaaS founder (or their company) could become a partner in a multi-disciplinary firm and align economics on attest work — though the audit-majority rule will likely persist, so a SaaS cannot *control* an audit firm.
2. **Cross-discipline delivery.** The SaaS can be the technology arm of an MDP delivering tax + legal + audit + advisory on one engagement, sharing the engagement fee internally without Clause (2) issues.
3. **M&A optionality.** A SaaS could roll up CA firms inside an MDP holding — but only if Companies-Act / LLP-Act amendments accompany the rules to permit corporate ownership. The current ICAI framework does *not* allow MDP to be constituted as a *company*; only as a partnership or LLP. Corporate ownership of CA practices is still off the table.

### 3.4 Realistic timeline

- Consultation closed 30 Sep 2025.
- IMG (inter-ministerial group) discussions started Q4 2025.
- Likely Cabinet/MCA notification: **late 2026 to mid-2027**.
- ICAI regulatory amendments to align (Regulation 53B, ethics code, member rules): **3-12 months after MCA notification**.
- Companies Act / LLP Act amendments (if needed for corporate ownership): **uncertain; could push effective date to 2028**.

**Bet for a 2026 SaaS founder:** Don't build a business model that depends on MDP. Build one that's MDP-*opportunistic* — i.e., works as a standalone SaaS today, and can add equity-share / partnership economics if the rules pass.

## 4. ICAI / NFRA Position on AI in Attest Engagements

### 4.1 Current state — no ICAI-specific AI guidance note

As of May 2026, ICAI has **not** issued a standalone "Guidance Note on Use of AI in Audit." The **13th edition Code of Ethics (effective 1 Apr 2026)** addresses AI obliquely: it expands the Management Consultancy and Other Services (MCS) definition to explicitly include "AI services," and the Code is aligned with the **IESBA 2024 edition** which incorporates technology-related amendments and a new Chapter 5 on Sustainability Assurance.

Practical position from ICAI public statements: AI is acknowledged, structured training is encouraged, **ethical use, transparency in client reporting, and retention of professional judgement** are emphasised. Translation: AI is a tool; the CA remains responsible.

### 4.2 SA 230 documentation and AI

The binding standard is **SA 230 (Audit Documentation)**. Key requirements:

- Documentation of nature/extent of audit procedures performed, audit evidence obtained, significant professional judgements, and discussions with management/TCWG.
- Individual who reviewed work and date/extent of review must be on the working paper.
- May be recorded on paper or electronic or other media (so AI-generated electronic working papers are permissible *in form*).
- Final audit file assembled within **60 days** of auditor's report; retained **7 years minimum**.
- For audit files *originally* in electronic form, must be preserved in that form (NFRA Circular 16 Dec 2025).

**Critical interpretation for AI tools:** SA 230 requires evidence that the auditor *reviewed* the work. AI-generated working papers without contemporaneous auditor review and sign-off are NFRA-bait. Any SaaS that produces AI-drafted working papers must capture (a) the auditor's review action and timestamp, (b) the prompt and source data used (audit trail), and (c) the version history. Without this, the auditor will lose if NFRA inspects.

### 4.3 NFRA on AI

NFRA in 2025 launched an "**IndiaAI Financial Reporting Compliance Challenge**" — an AI challenge to develop machine-learning tools for the *regulator itself* to detect financial-statement manipulation. This signals NFRA's posture: AI is welcome, *especially* if it improves audit quality and evidence trails. NFRA has NOT issued a circular restricting AI use by auditors.

However, the **16 Dec 2025 NFRA Circular NF-22/52/2025** on audit file maintenance is stringent. Several PIE audit firms have been penalised (see §5) for documentation gaps — exactly the area where AI tools either help (good audit trail) or hurt (un-reviewed AI outputs).

### 4.4 International comparison

| Regulator | AI guidance | Date | Posture |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PCAOB (US)** | Spotlight publication on GenAI in Audit | July 2024 | Information-gathering; 2025 inspections include AI areas; no standard yet |
| **FRC (UK)** | "Generative and Agentic AI in Audit" guidance | Mar 2026 | World-first specific AI audit guidance; principles-based; auditor accountability retained |
| **IAASB (global)** | Technology Position Statement + Audit Evidence project + 2025 Tech Quality Management initiative | Oct 2024 onward | No standalone AI standard; weaves AI into ISA 500, ISA 540, ISQM 1 |
| **ICAI (India)** | No standalone note; 13th edition Code references AI; ICAI AI Knowledge portal active | Apr 2026 | Soft encouragement; relies on existing SAs |
| **NFRA (India)** | No restrictive circular; uses AI itself for inspection | 2025-26 | Quietly favourable; insists on documentation discipline |

### 4.5 The structural risk

If NFRA penalises an AI-assisted audit, the order will not name the SaaS by brand — it will cite SA 230 / SQC 1 documentation failures. But press coverage will. **Mitigation for a SaaS vendor:**

- Force every AI output to be reviewed and signed off by the auditor before saving to the audit file.
- Maintain immutable audit trails of AI prompts, sources, and outputs.
- Provide a "review packet" that documents the auditor's professional judgement, not just the AI's output.
- Customer contracts should make clear the auditor is responsible for the audit conclusion, and the SaaS is a tool, not the auditor of record.

## 5. NFRA Enforcement Direction 2024-2026

### 5.1 Major orders

**BSR & Associates LLP — Coffee Day Enterprises FY18-19** (Aug 2024):
- ₹10 crore monetary penalty on firm — highest ever NFRA penalty
- 10-year debarment + ₹50 lakh penalty on engagement partner (Aravind Maiya)
- 5-year debarment + ₹25 lakh on EQCR partner (Amit Somani)
- Key findings: audit-paper modification without record; SQC 1 and SA 230 violations; failure to detect diversion of ₹3,535 crore to promoter entity.

**Venkatesh & Co. — Coffee Day FY19-20** (Oct 2024):
- ₹2 crore on firm; ₹15 lakh on two CAs
- Findings: accepted engagement before performing mandatory predecessor-auditor procedures; started audit before NOC from BSR & Associates.

**Lodha & Co. — Inspection Report 2023** (published 2024):
- Documentation gaps; risk-assessment failures; supervision issues.

**PwC Indian affiliates — 2024 NFRA inspections:**
- Deficiencies flagged at two PwC firms.

**Umamaheswara Rao & Co. — Audit Quality Inspection** (2025):
- Major lapses in documentation, supervision, and risk assessment.

### 5.2 The documentation gap as a SaaS wedge

NFRA's consistent finding across orders is **audit documentation failure** — workpapers modified without trail, missing review signatures, inadequate risk-assessment evidence, post-facto reconstruction. This is exactly what a well-built SaaS solves.

The wedge for a CA-tooling SaaS is **"NFRA-compliant audit documentation":** version-controlled workpapers, immutable review trails, automated checklists for SQC 1, prompt-and-output capture for any AI assistance, and a one-click NFRA-submission packet that meets the 7-day request response window.

### 5.3 NFRA's evolving expectations

- **Annual Transparency Reports** for PIE auditors (top 1000 listed companies first) — proposed; will create demand for compliance-reporting SaaS.
- **Audit Quality Maturity Model** referenced in inspection reports — firms now scored.
- **Mandatory training, monitoring, EQCR participation** documented — every CA firm with PIE clients now needs structured workflow tooling.

For a SaaS, the regulatory direction is *tailwind*: NFRA is making it more expensive to audit without good tooling.

## 6. ICAI CMP (Computerised Members in Practice) Listing

### 6.1 What it actually is

**CMP** stands for two different things on ICAI's website (confusingly):

1. **Committee for Members in Practice** — the ICAI committee under the Council that supports practitioners. URL: cmp.icai.org. Focus: capacity building, "Practice ki Paathshala," workshops.
2. **CMP Benefits Portal** (cmpbenefits.icai.org, redirects to cmp.icai.org/cmp-benefit/) — a curated list of software and services with discounted/free pricing for ICAI members in practice.

The CMP Benefits programme is what founders mean when they say "CMP listing." It is **not a certification**, **not an endorsement**, **not a regulatory approval**. It is a procurement-style empanelment: vendors offer discounted rates to ICAI members, ICAI lists them.

### 6.2 What is listed (verified May 2026)

Software currently on the CMP Benefits portal includes (categorised):

**Practice management & automation:**
- Simplify (practice management)
- MyMeet.io (appointment management)
- Click2Confirm.com (balance confirmations)
- Any Audit (forensic audits)
- EASYOFFICE (multi-functional platform)
- Antivirus protection facility

**Taxation & compliance:**
- VIDUR AI Assistant
- Assure AI (audit automation)
- Bill Mantra (GST audit)
- CA-TDSMAN (TDS filing)
- WrapTax (digital confirmations)
- KDK GST & TDS Software
- SPEQTA GST

**Accounting & billing:**
- Vyapar TaxOne (formerly **Suvit**) — verified
- My Bill Book
- Fynone
- Zoho Accounting

**Financial / banking:**
- RazorpayX
- NeSL IU for Audit
- Probe42

**Not verified on CMP as of search date:** **ClearTax** (despite the rumour — they may be on a separate enterprise procurement track; verify with cmp@icai.in). **Winman** (largest tax software in India; not seen on CMP listing in recent search results).

### 6.3 Application process

- ICAI does not publish a clear public RFP for CMP listing. Process is invitation/applicant-driven via cmp@icai.in.
- Vendor needs to offer a **discounted price for ICAI members** (typically 25-50% off market price).
- Vendor needs to **sustain the discount for the listing period**.
- ICAI does internal review (technical fit, vendor reputation, customer references). No published criteria, no published timeline.
- Typical time-to-listing: 3-9 months from first contact.
- No application fee, but ongoing marketing co-investment is expected (vendor may be asked to participate in webinars, branch events).

### 6.4 What CMP listing converts to

- **Distribution wedge:** the CMP portal is one of the few places an ICAI member actively looks for vetted software. Estimated active CA-firm visitors per month: low (under 50,000 unique members visiting the portal; the bulk discover software through peer/CPE/branch channels). So CMP traffic alone is modest.
- **Trust signal:** at sales conversation, "we are on CMP" closes a 5-10% trust gap with conservative tier-2/3 firms.
- **Branch/CPE access:** listed vendors are often invited to ICAI branch events and CPE webinars. This is real reach — ICAI runs ~2,000 CPE events/year.
- **Not** a regulatory approval. **Not** a guarantee of revenue. **Not** a moat.

### 6.5 Pursue it?

**Yes — once you have product-market fit (≥ ₹3-5 crore ARR) and can sustain the discount.** Pursuing it before then is premature; the discount you'd offer would erode unit economics and you'd be invisible on the portal among 30+ vendors. Pursue it as a **trust-and-distribution layer** rather than a launch strategy.

## 7. DPDPA Compliance for CA-Firm Data

### 7.1 The legal stack

- **Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023** — enacted Aug 2023.
- **DPDP Rules, 2025** — notified **13 Nov 2025** (G.S.R. 846(E) and adjacent).
- Phasing: Rules 1, 2, 17-21 effective immediately; Rule 4 (Consent Manager registration) after 1 year (~Nov 2026); substantive Rules with **18-month transition → effective ~13 May 2027**.

### 7.2 Data residency for CA-firm data

DPDPA does **not** mandate blanket data localisation. Section 16 lets the central government **blacklist** specific countries — default is "transfer permitted unless restricted."

However:
- **RBI mandates** (Oct 2018) require payment system data stored in India.
- **IRDAI mandates** require insurance data stored in India.
- For CA-firm data specifically (client ledgers, tax data, audit working papers): **no explicit DPDPA localisation requirement**. But:
  - PIE audit working papers must remain producible to NFRA on 7 days' notice (NFRA Circular 16 Dec 2025) — practically requires Indian-region storage.
  - Bank-audit working papers (RBI/IBA empanelment) carry contractual India-residency expectations.
  - Most enterprise CA-firm customers will demand **AWS Mumbai region** or equivalent (Azure India, GCP Mumbai) as a procurement requirement, irrespective of strict legal need.

**Net recommendation:** Pick AWS Mumbai or Azure India Central. Cost increment vs. US regions: ~15-25% on compute, similar on storage. Worth it for the procurement-friction reduction alone.

### 7.3 Consent for processing CA client PII / financial data

DPDP applies to **personal data of natural persons**. Most CA-firm data is *corporate* — entity GSTRs, ITRs of companies, audit data — which is **not personal data** under DPDP. Personal data shows up in:

- Individual ITRs, partner-level data, employee payroll, KYC documents
- Director / promoter PANs, Aadhaar copies
- HR data of CA-firm employees and trainees

For these, the CA firm is the **Data Fiduciary**, the individual is the **Data Principal**. The SaaS vendor is a **Data Processor** acting on the CA firm's behalf. The CA firm needs explicit consent from data principals.

**Vendor obligation under DPDP Rules 2025:**
- Process only on documented instructions from the Data Fiduciary
- Notify breaches without undue delay
- Sub-processor controls and contracts
- Help Data Fiduciary respond to data principal requests
- Encryption / masking / tokenisation as appropriate
- Logging with 1-year retention minimum

### 7.4 Breach notification — 72 hours

Under DPDP Rules 2025:
- **Initial intimation to Data Protection Board: within 72 hours** of detecting breach.
- Detailed follow-up report (causes, mitigation, affected principals, prevention measures).
- **Direct notification to affected Data Principals** with breach description, likely consequences, mitigation, and safety advice.

This bites the **CA firm** (Data Fiduciary), not the SaaS, but the SaaS contractually owes the CA firm immediate notification (typically within 24 hours of detection) to enable the CA to meet the 72-hour deadline.

### 7.5 DPO requirement

- **Mandatory for Significant Data Fiduciaries (SDFs).** Section 10(2)(a).
- SDF designation by Central Government notification (not automatic); criteria include volume, sensitivity, decision-making impact.
- Anticipated SDF threshold (not yet officially notified): processing personal data of ≥ 50 lakh Indian residents.

A CA-tooling SaaS at scale (handling personal data of ≥ 50 lakh individuals through customer CA firms) may be designated SDF and require:
- Indian-resident DPO
- Annual Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)
- Independent annual audit
- Algorithmic / AI-system due diligence
- Submit audit reports to the Board

### 7.6 Penalties

- **Up to ₹250 crore** for failure to implement reasonable security safeguards.
- **Up to ₹200 crore** for failure to notify the Board or data principals of a breach.
- **Up to ₹200 crore** for child-data violations.
- Graduated penalties for MSMEs.

### 7.7 Practical compliance — what does it cost a SaaS?

| Item | Indicative cost (₹/year) |
|---|---|
| **ISO 27001 certification** (initial) | ₹3-5 lakh; ₹1-2 lakh annual surveillance |
| **SOC 2 Type II** (annual) | ₹15-25 lakh (USD 18-30k) |
| **DPDP-specific gap assessment + remediation** | ₹3-8 lakh one-time, ₹2-4 lakh annual |
| **Compliance automation platform** (Sprinto / Scrut / Drata / Delve) | ₹6-12 lakh/year |
| **DPO retainer** (if SDF) | ₹8-15 lakh/year (part-time) or ₹25-40 lakh full-time |
| **Cyber-insurance** (₹10 cr coverage) | ₹3-6 lakh/year |
| **Encryption / KMS / WAF infra** | ₹1-3 lakh/year |
| **Combined (ISO+SOC2+DPDP+automation) bundle** | ₹15-25 lakh/year for a startup |

For an early-stage CA-tooling SaaS, the realistic compliance budget is **₹10-15 lakh/year** until enterprise customers force the upgrade to ISO+SOC2+DPDP-audited posture (typically at ₹3-5 cr ARR).

### 7.8 DPDPA compliance checklist (10 items) for a CA-tooling SaaS

1. **Indian-region hosting**: AWS Mumbai / Azure India Central as primary; geo-redundancy within India.
2. **Data Processing Agreement (DPA)** with every CA-firm customer: instructions, sub-processor list, breach notification (24-hour internal), audit rights.
3. **Encryption**: at-rest (AES-256), in-transit (TLS 1.3); customer-managed keys for enterprise tiers.
4. **Consent capture**: where the SaaS itself collects PII from data principals (e.g., client portal for CA firm's clients), explicit, granular, plain-language consent with withdrawal flow.
5. **Breach response playbook**: 24/7 SOC monitoring, defined sev levels, automated 24-hour customer notification, 72-hour Board notification logistics pre-rehearsed.
6. **Retention and erasure**: configurable per-customer retention policies; automated deletion workflows; 48-hour advance erasure notice; audit-engagement retention ≥ 7 years overriding consumer-side deletion when applicable.
7. **Access control**: SSO/SAML, role-based access, MFA, just-in-time access for support, complete access logs retained 1+ year.
8. **DPIA**: at first product launch and on every significant feature change involving new PII categories.
9. **DPO appointment**: India-resident DPO (or fractional) registered with the Data Protection Board if/when designated SDF.
10. **Annual third-party audit**: ISO 27001 minimum at scale; SOC 2 Type II by ₹5 cr ARR; DPDP compliance attestation by 13 May 2027.
