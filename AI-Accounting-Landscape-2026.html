TL;DR — Five Things Worth Knowing

1. Capital is funding platforms-into-firms, not firms themselves. Basis hit a $1.15B valuation in Feb 2026 selling AI into accounting firms. Fieldguide raised $75M Series C at $700M selling audit tooling. Aiwyn raised $113M Series B from KKR & Bessemer for practice-management/AI tax. Meanwhile Bench shut down in Dec 2024 with 12,000 customers stranded, and Botkeeper went under in Feb 2026 after $90M raised. The "AI bookkeeping firm" graveyard is real.
2. The incumbents are no longer asleep. Intuit Assist became the default for all US QBO customers by Sept 2025. Xero's "Just Ask Xero" shipped Sep 2025 after the $70M Syft acquisition. Sage Copilot has 40,000+ early adopters. CCH Axcess Expert AI rolled embedded agents across the entire stack in Oct 2025. The window for "we have AI, they don't" closes in 12–18 months.
3. Three structural tailwinds are not hype — they're demography. ~300K accountants have exited the profession since 2020 (BLS). Only 1.4% of college students chose accounting in 2023 (vs. ~4% a decade ago). 11 states have already passed alternative-pathway CPA licensure laws, with 14 more pending. Something has to fill the gap.
4. PE is the silent giant. 170 PE accounting-firm transactions in 2025 — more than 2× the prior year. PE now holds stakes in 10 of the top 30 US CPA firms. Six dominant rollup platforms: Ascend, Ryan, Crete, Aprio, Sorren, Doeren Mayhew. If you build a firm, your exit is more likely PE than VC.
5. The "AI Accounting Firm for Startups" wedge is dangerously crowded. Pilot, Zeni, Truewind, Mesh, Afternoon, Finta, Puzzle, Balance, Minerva, Cranston all chase the same VC-backed-startup ICP. Most are sub-$1M ARR with no clear differentiation beyond "AI + CPA review." Pilot itself is at ~$27M revenue on $222M raised — a slow grind, not a rocket. If this is your wedge, you're showing up to a knife fight with 12 other knives.

Why Now — Four Forces Compounding

1. The supply-side collapse is demographic, not cyclical.

BLS shows ~300K accountants gone since 2020 (~17% of the workforce). Only 1.4% of college students chose accounting in 2023. CPA Exam candidates fell from 72,271 (2021) to 67,335 (2022) — the lowest since 2006. BLS still projects ~136,400 annual openings through 2034. Even if AI doesn't fully work, something must fill the gap.

2. The 150-hour rule is finally bending.

NASBA + AICPA updated the Uniform Accountancy Act in 2025 to add a 120-hour bachelor's + 2 years experience pathway. Ohio first to sign (HB 238, effective Jan 2026). By May 2025, 11 states had passed alternative pathways; 14 more pending. A bigger licensable pool is coming, but slowly.

3. PE is consolidating the demand side.

170 PE accounting-firm deals in 2025 — 2× prior year. PE now in 10 of top 30 US CPA firms. Ascend alone made 40 acquisitions since Jan 2023 (18 in 2025), reaching $590M net revenue. Carr Riggs & Ingram took Centerbridge/Bessemer money and started rolling up. The buyer for an AI-native firm is increasingly a PE platform, not a strategic.

4. Model capability hit the agentic inflection.

Sequoia's Julien Bek: "$1 of software → $6 of services to make it work." Foundation Capital, Bessemer, and a16z all published the "service as software" thesis in 2024–25. Bessemer pegs the vertical AI opportunity at 10× vertical SaaS — addressing the 13% of US GDP spent on business labor vs. the 1% on IT. Numeric, Basis, Fieldguide, Ramp's agents all materially shipped in this window.

The 24-Month M&A & Distress Timeline

The pattern is sharp: AI-native + profitable → up rounds. 2021-vintage spend/FP&A SaaS → down exits, take-privates, debt rounds. Two "AI bookkeeping firm" deaths bracket the period.

Two graveyards, one cluster: Bench (Dec '24, $100M+ raised, 12K customers) and Botkeeper (Feb '26, $90M raised, 11 years operating) both shut down after explicitly being "the AI bookkeeping firm at scale." They didn't fail because AI didn't work — they failed because SMB unit economics don't tolerate 35–55% gross margins at $300–$700/mo ACV against churn. If you're building #15 in this graveyard, plan for unit economics from day one, not customer acquisition.

Strategic Implications — Six Decisions to Make Up Front

1. Pick your game: Firm vs. Platform-to-Firms vs. Software.

The three games have different rules, capital efficiency, and exits.

  • Be the firm (service): 35–55% gross margins, regulatory exposure, slow ramp, PE exit. Pilot at $27M revenue on $222M raised is the cautionary tale. Bench/Botkeeper are the graveyard.
  • Sell platform to firms (picks-and-shovels): 70–85% gross margins, $50K–$500K+ ACVs at top firms, strategic VC capital. Basis $1.15B, Fieldguide $700M, Aiwyn $240M, Karbon ~$100M, Canopy $70M Series C. This is where the money is in 2026.
  • Software direct (SMB): Compete with Intuit Assist, Xero JAX, Sage Copilot, Ramp's Accounting Agent — all of which ship for free or near-free. Path is brutal without a moat.

2. If you go "be the firm," pick a vertical or your unit economics die.

The horizontal play is dead. Two playbooks survive: (a) vertical depth (Heard for therapists, Lettuce for solopreneurs, Adaptive for construction) where you can charge a premium AND your AI can learn industry-specific GAAP; (b) geographic depth (Bluebook in Nordics) where competition is thin and CAC is lower. Vertical TAMs are smaller but margins are 2–3× higher and churn is half.

3. Take the demography tailwind seriously — but don't bet the company on it.

300K accountants gone since 2020 is real. 1.4% of college students choosing accounting is real. 11 states bending the 150-hour rule is real. But India + Philippines fills 56.5% of global accounting delivery today. The supply gap doesn't translate to your pricing power if Entigrity offers a 200K-person bench at one-third the cost. Plan AI as a margin-additive layer on top of offshore labor, not as a replacement. Most successful "AI firms" today are AI-enabled offshore teams — don't pretend otherwise on your pitch deck.

4. The Ramp threat is bigger than the Pilot threat.

Pilot is your direct competitor on the surface, but Ramp is your existential threat. Ramp's Accounting Agent (Feb 2026) ships free with the card, auto-codes 100% of transactions, claims 98% accuracy. SMBs that adopt Ramp Cards + Accounting Agent get bookkeeping bundled. Your differentiation can't be "we do bookkeeping with AI" — Ramp does that already, for free. Your differentiation has to be the things Ramp deliberately doesn't do: complex multi-entity, tax filing, audit prep, advisory, vertical-specific GAAP.

5. Build for the PE exit, not the VC exit.

170 PE accounting-firm deals in 2025. Six dominant rollup platforms (Ascend, Ryan, Crete, Aprio, Sorren, Doeren Mayhew). If you build a firm with $5–20M ARR, your buyer is more likely an Ascend than a strategic. Optimize for: clean books (irony), high-margin services mix, recurring revenue contracts, regional density (rollups buy adjacencies). Avoid: VC-style cash burn, complex tech debt only your team understands, founder dependency.

6. Lean into the picks-and-shovels playbook — but find a wedge not already won.

Basis ($1.15B) and Fieldguide ($700M) didn't show up first; they showed up best. The capital is real but the field is filling. Where there's still wedge space: (a) AI agents for the long-tail of 4,500+ firms that Aiwyn/Karbon/Canopy don't serve well; (b) cross-border tax/audit tooling (most platforms are US-only); (c) specialty practice areas (R&D credit, transfer pricing, sales-and-use); (d) "AI for the AI firms" — infrastructure that other AI bookkeeping firms run on. Juno is doing this for tax data extraction; the model can be extended to bank feed normalization, journal entry AI, payroll-to-GL mapping.

Trap to avoid: "AI for accounting" without a verb.

If your pitch is "we use AI to do accounting," you have nothing. The winning pitches have a verb that names the work: Black Ore prepares tax returns. Petual tests SOX controls. Numeric closes the books. Filed processes returns. Fieldguide manages audit engagements. Pick the verb, ship the agent, win the wedge. Then expand.
