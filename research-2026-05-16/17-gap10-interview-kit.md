# Gap 10 — Customer Discovery Interview Kit for Indian CA Firm Partners

**Prepared for:** the founder (no accounting background, warm CA network)
**Date compiled:** 2026-05-16
**Purpose:** Run 30 customer discovery interviews across 6 weeks with Indian CA partners to identify the sharpest wedge among 5 candidate verbs without contaminating signal by pitching.
**Methodological backbone:** "The Mom Test" (Rob Fitzpatrick), "Talking to Humans" (Giff Constable), Y Combinator customer-discovery canon, behavioral signals over stated intent.

---

## SECTION 1 — INTERVIEW KIT OVERVIEW

### 1.1 The 60-minute structure

| Minutes | Block                                  | Purpose                                           |
| ------- | -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| 0–3     | Warm-up                                | Reciprocity, kill the "you're selling me" defense |
| 3–10    | "Tell me about last week" calibration  | Surface what they actually do                     |
| 10–30   | 5-verb probe (4 mins × 5 verbs)        | Comparative pain ranking                          |
| 30–40   | Competitive landscape                  | What they've tried, why it failed                 |
| 40–50   | Workaround inventory + "worst version" | Find hack-builders and severity anchors           |
| 50–55   | First-deliverable test                 | Find people willing to bleed for a fix today      |
| 55–60   | Asks, intros, scheduling               | Convert warm-warm intros                          |

The interview is **partner-only**. Manager interviews are useful as a follow-up but they describe the work, not the buying decision. The partner is the buyer in 95% of cases.

### 1.2 Sample composition (30 interviews)

| City tier | Cities | Solo CA | Small firm (3–15 staff) | Mid-tier partner | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 | Mumbai, Bangalore, Delhi | 3 | 5 | 3 | 11 |
| Tier 1.5 | Pune, Hyderabad, Chennai, Ahmedabad | 4 | 6 | 2 | 12 |
| Tier 2 | Indore, Jaipur, Coimbatore, Lucknow | 3 | 4 | 0 | 7 |
| **Total** | | **10** | **15** | **5** | **30** |

**Stratification rules:**
- At least 4 partners with > 20 GST-monthly clients (proxy for reconciliation pain).
- At least 6 partners doing > 30 tax audits / year (Form 3CD relevance).
- At least 3 partners doing GST scrutiny / appellate work (notice reply relevance).
- At least 2 partners with NO statutory audit practice (negative control; surfaces wrong-segment quickly).
- Decline interviews where the firm is purely a tax-filing shop (ITR-1/2 only) — wrong economics for the candidate wedges.

### 1.3 The "Don't Tip Your Hat" Principle

The founder must NOT:
- Say the words "AI", "automation", "agent", "co-pilot" anywhere in the first 50 minutes.
- Describe what he's building. If asked "what's your product?", reply: "Honestly I haven't built it yet — I'm 6 weeks into figuring out what CAs actually need. Mind if I just ask questions today?"
- Volunteer hypotheses. If the partner says "we waste so much time on reconciliation", don't say "yes that's exactly why I'm here" — say "tell me about the last reconciliation that went badly."
- Pitch even at the end. If the partner asks "so what will you build?", reply: "Too early. The 30 conversations are the input. I'd love to come back to you with options in 8 weeks."
- Use accounting jargon he doesn't fully own. Wrong jargon kills credibility faster than admitting ignorance.

**Why this matters:** The founder has no accounting background. Once he pitches, the partner becomes polite. Polite signal is fatal — it confirms whatever the founder already wants to hear. Behavioral specifics survive politeness; abstract statements don't.

### 1.4 Recording, consent, note-taking

- Ask for permission to record at minute 0. ~80% of CAs will say yes if framed as "I want to listen back, not share."
- If they decline, take handwritten notes; do not type. Eye contact > completeness.
- Within 6 hours, fill the post-interview note template (Section 9.2). Memory decays fast and contaminates the next interview.

## SECTION 2 — THE "TELL ME ABOUT LAST WEEK" CALIBRATION QUESTION

### 2.1 The script

> "Before we get into anything specific, I'd love to just hear about your week — like literally what happened from last Monday to Sunday. Walk me through it. What time did you get to office Monday? What's the first thing you opened on your laptop?"

Then **shut up**. Average partner will speak 4-7 minutes uninterrupted if prompted well.

### 2.2 Why it works

This question is the single highest-leverage calibration tool in the kit because:
1. It anchors on **observed past behavior** (Mom Test #1 rule), not stated preference.
2. It reveals time allocation **organically** — what they spent hours on shows up; what they only complain about doesn't.
3. It surfaces **invisible work** — chasing clients on WhatsApp, scrutiny replies they didn't plan for, partner meetings about staffing.
4. It establishes the founder as a listener, not a salesperson.

### 2.3 Probes during the walkthrough

When they say something vague, **ask for the concrete instance**:

| They say | You ask |
|---|---|
| "Spent the morning on GST stuff" | "Which client? What did the morning look like minute by minute?" |
| "Had to deal with a notice" | "What kind of notice? Section? Whose client? What did you do first?" |
| "Article was struggling" | "Struggling with what specifically? What did you have to redo?" |
| "Long day" | "When did you leave? What was the last thing you did?" |

### 2.4 Seasonal variants

**Pre-interview, find out the partner's "season":**
- **Off-season (Jun, Nov-Dec):** ask about a normal week. This is the truth-week.
- **Busy season (Jul-Sept ITR/3CD, Apr-May year-end, monthly 10th-20th GST):** ask about a normal week AND a recent crunch week — get both.
- **Specifically post-Sept-30** (just after tax audit deadline): ask "tell me about the worst day of September." This is the highest-emotion, highest-truth recall.

### 2.5 What "good" looks like

A high-quality answer reveals at least:
- Hour-blocked time allocation for 3+ workdays.
- 2+ named clients.
- 1+ specific compliance deliverable (e.g., "Tuesday I sat with Rahul on the ABC Pvt Ltd GSTR-2B").
- An unprompted complaint (e.g., "the GST portal was down again Wednesday").

If the answer stays abstract for >2 minutes, the partner is being defensive or has poor recall. Redirect: "Sorry, can you back up — what was Monday actually like?"

## SECTION 3 — THE 5-VERB PROBE (4 minutes × 5 verbs = 20 minutes)

### 3.1 Verb list (in interview order)

Order matters. Start with the lowest-emotion verb (GST monthly) so the partner warms up, end with the highest-emotion verb (scrutiny notice reply) where the candor peaks.

1. **GST monthly return** (GSTR-1 + 3B): high-frequency baseline.
2. **GSTR-2B reconciliation**: the article-killer.
3. **Form 3CD (tax audit)**: annual peak pain.
4. **Audit working paper drafting**: skill-heavy, season-bound.
5. **Scrutiny notice reply**: highest-emotion, most variable.

### 3.2 The 4-question template (applied to each verb)

**Q1 — Severity (1 min, ask open):**
> "What's the most annoying thing about [verb] today?"

Then **be quiet for 30 seconds**. The first answer is usually surface; the second answer after silence is the real one. Never suggest. If they say "it's fine", probe: "When was the last time it wasn't fine?"

**Q2 — Frequency + actual time (1 min, ask specific):**
> "How many times a month/year do you do this? And — honestly — how long does it really take? Not the cleaned-up answer, the actual answer."

The phrase "not the cleaned-up answer" is critical. CAs anchor on the time they'd quote to a client, not the real time. Probe further: "Including the chasing of documents? Including the back-and-forth with the partner? Including filing-day portal retries?"

**Q3 — Current solution (1.5 min, ask narrative):**
> "What did you do the LAST time this came up? Walk me through it. What did you open, who did you ping, what tool did you use, what was annoying?"

This is the single highest-information question in the kit. Listen for:
- **Tool names** (Tally, ClearTax, Winman, Computax, KDK Spectrum, Suvit, etc.).
- **Channel switching** (WhatsApp → Excel → portal → email).
- **Manual workarounds** (custom Excel macros, Sheets templates, intern scripts).
- **Hand-offs** (who passed work to whom and when it broke).

If they say "we just do it in Excel", probe: "Can you share the Excel template? Not now — later." 90% will agree. That template is gold.

**Q4 — WTP signal (1 min, ask economic):**
> "How much do you charge the client for this? And separately — how much have you spent on tools / software / staff to do it well?"

If they conflate the two, separate them: "I get the fee, but specifically on tools — what did you pay last year for software that helps with this?"

**Output format** for each verb (record in spreadsheet):
- Severity raw score 1–5
- Times per month/year + real hours per cycle
- Current toolchain + workaround inventory
- Fee charged to client + tooling spend

### 3.3 Per-verb interview crib sheet

These notes are **for the founder to read before the interview, not to recite**. They are the technical priors that prevent the founder from missing a tell.

#### Verb A — GST monthly return (GSTR-1 + 3B)
- **Cycle:** 11th (GSTR-1) → 14th (2B drops) → 20th (3B). 12× / year per GSTIN.
- **Time priors:** 2-6 hrs per client per month at a small firm.
- **Fee priors:** ₹500-2,500/mo per GSTIN at small firms; ₹3,000-8,000 at mid-tier.
- **Listen for:** "IMS dashboard", "supplier didn't upload", "portal timeout at 11:30 PM", "hard-locked Table 4", "credit reversal under Rule 42/43".
- **Negative tell:** If they say "GST is easy, the article handles it" — wedge is in the wrong place for this firm.

#### Verb B — GSTR-2B reconciliation
- **Cycle:** Every month, 14th-19th window. Hardest single recurring task in the office.
- **Time priors:** 1-8 hrs per client per month depending on vendor count; 4 junior accountants full-time at firms with 100+ clients.
- **Listen for:** "invoice number doesn't match", "vendor uploaded late", "ITC reversal", "RCM mismatch", "ineligible credit", "credit note timing", "import IGST".
- **Existing tools:** Suvit, CORAA, ClearMax ITC, Tally's own 2B recon. Each has gaps.
- **Strong wedge signal:** the partner says "we have an Excel template we built ourselves" — they've invested unpaid hours; the pain is binding.

#### Verb C — Form 3CD (tax audit)
- **Cycle:** Annual, September 30 deadline. 1× per tax-audit client. Volume per firm: 20-200 clients.
- **Time priors:** 8-30 hrs per client; senior+partner heavy.
- **Fee priors:** ₹15,000-50,000 at small firms; ₹75K-5L at mid-tier (turnover-linked).
- **Listen for:** Clause 21 (related-party disallowances), Clause 27 (CENVAT carry-forward), Clause 34 (TDS detail), Clause 44 (GST vs accounts reconciliation — the new beast), Clause 18 (depreciation per ICDS).
- **Strong wedge signal:** the partner expresses dread about a specific named audit ("the ABC Pvt Ltd 3CD is going to kill me this September").

#### Verb D — Audit working paper drafting (statutory audit)
- **Cycle:** Year-round but peaks Apr-Sept. Per engagement: 1-3 months elapsed time.
- **Time priors:** 40-400 article-hours per engagement at small firm; 200-2,000 at mid-tier.
- **Fee priors:** ₹15,000-35,000 (small Pvt Ltd statutory) → ₹50K-15L (mid-tier corporate).
- **Listen for:** "CARO 2020", "Ind AS adjustments", "tie-out to trial balance", "ratios", "going concern", "review notes from the partner that come back at 11 PM".
- **Negative tell:** If they say "we don't do statutory audit — only tax audits" — they're not the audit-WP customer.

#### Verb E — Scrutiny notice reply (income tax)
- **Cycle:** Variable. Triggered by AIS/26AS mismatch, Section 143(2) selection, faceless assessment.
- **Time priors:** 8-40 hrs per notice elapsed; spreads over 2-6 weeks.
- **Fee priors:** ₹10K-50K small firm; ₹50K-3L mid-tier per matter.
- **Listen for:** "faceless", "DRP", "ITAT", "AIS mismatch", "Section 143(2)", "Section 143(1) intimation", "S.148 reassessment".
- **Strong wedge signal:** partner has a recent visceral example. Notices are emotional. If they tell a 4-minute story unprompted — this is the wedge.

### 3.4 Two cross-verb calibration questions

After all 5 verbs, ask:

**Calibration #1 — Delegation reality:**
> "For each of these five things — who *actually* does the work? You? A manager? An article? Why?"

A partner who personally does GSTR-2B reconciliation = either small firm or strong control-freak (both are buyer profiles). A partner who delegates all 5 = pure firm-builder; tooling buys are committee decisions; harder sale.

**Calibration #2 — The worst-version anchor:**
> "What's the worst version of [verb that just got highest severity] that's happened to you in the last 6 months? Tell me the story."

Story-format anchored on a recent specific event gets you the **emotional truth**. A 4-minute story with named clients, named staff, named amounts of money = wedge candidate. A 30-second generic answer = the pain is theoretical.

## SECTION 4 — THE COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE PROBE (10 minutes)

### 4.1 Core questions

**Q1 — Most recent adoption:**
> "When did you last try a new software tool — anything related to your practice? Walk me through how that decision happened."

Probes:
- Who suggested it? (Partner, peer, son, vendor cold call?)
- What was the trigger? (Pain or curiosity?)
- Who decided? (Sole decision or committee?)
- How long did the trial last?

**Q2 — Most recent abandonment (most important question in this section):**
> "What's the last tool you stopped using? Why?"

Listen for failure modes:
- "Didn't integrate with Tally" — integration gap.
- "Sales guy disappeared after the demo" — vendor support gap.
- "Couldn't get the article to use it" — change-management gap.
- "Started cheap, then they hiked the price" — pricing-trust gap.
- "We forgot about it" — adoption-curve gap.

Abandonment stories are the highest-information signal in this section because they describe **what makes Indian CAs reject software**, which is the launch playbook in reverse.

**Q3 — Peer benchmarking:**
> "What's the cleverest thing your peer firm at [X city or alumni circle] is doing on operations? Or who do you think runs the best practice in your circle?"

This surfaces:
- Names of admired firms (potential interviewees).
- Names of admired techniques (potential wedges they think are valuable).
- Their self-positioning ("we're traditional vs. modern").

**Q4 — Vendor recognition probe:**
> "Have you heard of Suvit? QwikCA? ClearTax for CA-firms? CORAA? Karbon? TaxDome? What do you think of each?"

Mention them in random order; don't cluster competitors together. Listen for **specifics vs. vague**:
- "Suvit — I tried the WhatsApp data collection thing, it was OK but my clients on iPhone hated it" = informed opinion.
- "Yeah I think I've heard of them" = no opinion = not a sophisticated buyer; might still buy but won't be a useful design partner.

### 4.2 Anti-vanity check

**Do NOT** ask:
- "If a product did X, would you pay for it?" (Hypothetical → useless.)
- "What features would you want?" (Solution mode → useless.)
- "What's your AI strategy?" (CEO theatre → useless.)

These are the Mom Test cardinal sins and the founder will be tempted to ask all three when energy flags around minute 35. Memorize the alternatives.

## SECTION 5 — THREE SIGNALS OF A USEFUL EARLY ADOPTER

The goal of 30 interviews is not to find 30 customers — it's to find **3-5 design partners**. The signals below distinguish a design partner from a polite respondent.

### 5.1 Positive signal #1: The unpaid hack workaround

**What it looks like:**
- A custom Excel macro for 2B matching that the partner built or paid an intern to build.
- A Google Sheets template shared across the firm with conditional formatting for ITC mismatch flags.
- A Power Query / Python script run by the firm's "tech-savvy" article.
- A WhatsApp Business broadcast list with bespoke templates for client document chasing.

**Why it matters:** The partner has invested **unpaid hours or stipend dollars** into mitigating this pain. This is the strongest indicator of binding constraint. If the pain weren't binding, the workaround wouldn't exist — they'd just live with it.

**Probe to surface it:** "Have you guys built any Excel templates or shortcuts for this internally? Can I see one?" 60-70% of partners with the pain have a workaround. 30-40% will share the template.

### 5.2 Positive signal #2: A named upcoming deliverable

**What it looks like:**
- "The Form 3CD for ABC Pvt Ltd is due Sept 30 and I'm dreading it because…"
- "We have a Section 143(2) hearing on the Mehta Group on June 12 and the reply is half-done."
- "The bank audit for XYZ Cooperative starts July 1 and I have 4 articles and 11 branches."

**Why it matters:** Concrete future-anchored pain is buying intent. A partner with a named deadline in the next 90 days is in a buying window. A partner with no named deadlines is in a learning window — useful for research, not for design partnership.

**Probe to surface it:** End of severity question for each verb — "Is there a specific upcoming one that's worrying you?"

### 5.3 Positive signal #3: The follow-up question

**What it looks like:**
- "Wait — how do you think about [problem]?"
- "What have other CAs told you?"
- "Why are you looking at this and not at GST audit / forensic / whatever?"
- "Can I introduce you to my friend at [other firm]?"

**Why it matters:** Genuine curiosity = the partner sees the founder as a peer thinker, not a vendor. Polite nodding = the partner sees a sales pitch coming and is being kind. The follow-up question is the cleanest behavioral signal of design-partner potential.

### 5.4 Anti-signals (deprioritize)

- **"Yes, that would be great"** with no specifics. Polite. Tells you nothing.
- **"AI is the future, you should definitely build something"** — generic future-tense compliments. Mom Test red flag #1.
- **"Send me a demo when ready"** — soft deflection; partner doesn't believe you'll ship.
- **"My son works in tech, he says AI will change everything"** — partner is performing modernity, not signaling buying intent.
- **Asks for pricing in the first 20 minutes** — wants to disqualify you on cost before engaging on pain. Polite-exit signal.

### 5.5 Composite scoring

After each interview, score on a binary basis:
- Hack workaround present? Y/N
- Named upcoming deliverable? Y/N
- Follow-up question asked? Y/N
- 0 anti-signals raised? Y/N

A partner with ≥3 of 4 is a design-partner candidate. Expect 4-7 out of 30 to qualify. **This is the right number — don't try to make all 30 fit.**

## SECTION 6 — THE "NO" RESPONSES THAT FLIP THE THESIS

Some interview responses are **structural disqualifiers** — they don't just reduce one firm's fit, they invalidate the wedge thesis. The founder must hear and accept these without trying to "convert" the partner.

### 6.1 The five thesis-flipping "no" patterns

| Response | What it means | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **"I just tell my articles to do it"** | Labor is cheap and abundant for this firm. The verb is not yet a binding constraint. | Don't try to sell. Ask: "What stops you from giving them MORE work?" — surfaces the actual constraint. |
| **"I charge ₹500 for that"** | The fee economics are below the floor that any subscription software can capture. | Software at ₹2,000+/month/seat is unsellable here. Either move upmarket or attack a different verb. |
| **"We don't take that kind of work"** | Wrong service mix. The firm has self-selected away from this verb. | Useful negative data. Skip the rest of the probe for this verb; move on. |
| **"I've tried 3 tools, none worked"** | Either the entire category is broken OR this partner is the problem (not the tools). | Probe the abandonment in depth (Section 4 Q2). If specifics are coherent → category insight. If specifics are vague → it's the partner. |
| **"I'd buy if my partner agreed"** | Consensus-blocker firm; not a sole-decision buyer. | Ask: "Can you set up a call with your partner?" If yes → useful. If "let me check" → low-priority pipeline. |

### 6.2 The "I'm too busy to evaluate" no

A surprisingly common rejection. It looks like buying intent ("I love what you're doing!") but ends with "let me see what you build and come back to me in 6 months."

**Interpretation:** This is a polite no, not a yes. The partner is signaling that pain is below the threshold required to switch tools. Don't waste a design-partner slot here. File as "stay-in-touch", not "pipeline".

### 6.3 The wedge-flipping aggregate

If after 10 interviews:
- 5+ partners say "articles handle it" for a given verb → that verb is not the wedge.
- 5+ partners give fees < ₹2,000/cycle for a given verb → economics don't support SaaS at that verb.
- 5+ partners cite a single competitor positively (e.g., "Suvit handles that") → the verb is occupied; move on.

The founder must **commit ahead of time** to these triggers. Otherwise confirmation bias will rationalize each "no" as an exception.

## SECTION 7 — THE FIRST-DELIVERABLE TEST

This is the most critical question in the kit. The other 50 minutes are research; this question is **commitment**.

### 7.1 The framing

> "Look — I know you're busy. If I came back to you in 2 weeks with a thing that did [X] on one of your actual clients — would you spend 15 minutes telling me what's wrong with it? Not buying it, not committing to anything — just review it and tell me where it falls over?"

The framing is **maximally low-friction** so the friction itself becomes the signal. A partner who can't spare 15 minutes for a hands-on review is not going to be a customer at any price.

### 7.2 Variants by firm size

#### Solo CA variant
> "Send me 3 invoices from your toughest client — toughest meaning the most messy, not the biggest. I'll come back to you in 48 hours with what an AI would have categorized in GST and what it would have flagged. You spend 10 minutes telling me where it's wrong. Deal?"

Success metric: partner sends the 3 invoices within 72 hours of the call.

#### Small firm (3-15 staff) variant
> "Give me a Tally backup of one client — pick the messiest one. I'll come back in 7 days with the GSTR-2B reconciliation for the last filed month, side by side with what your team filed. You and your senior spend 30 minutes telling me where I'd embarrass you. Deal?"

Success metric: partner sends the Tally backup within 14 days, AND blocks 30 minutes for review.

#### Mid-tier partner variant
> "Pick one of your engagement managers. Let's pilot on 5 clients for 30 days. I'll personally configure everything. Your manager gives me 1 hour a week of feedback — that's the only commitment. After 30 days, you decide whether it goes to 25 clients or to the bin."

Success metric: partner introduces the manager to the founder within 14 days, AND signs a 30-day no-commercial-terms pilot letter.

### 7.3 Why this works

The Mom Test calls this "the commitment-and-advancement axis". Words are free; commitments cost something. A real customer:
- Sends data.
- Blocks calendar time.
- Introduces team members.
- Says yes within 14 days, not "let me think about it."

A polite respondent does none of these. The first-deliverable test sorts the two within 2 weeks of the interview.

### 7.4 What to do if they say no to the deliverable

DO NOT try to overcome the objection. Instead say:
> "Totally fair. Genuinely curious — what would have to be true for you to say yes to something like this?"

The answer to that question is the **real adoption barrier**. Examples:
- "I'd need to see ICAI sign off on this" → regulatory anchor.
- "I'd need my managing partner to OK it" → consensus blocker.
- "Honestly, this isn't the worst pain we have" → wrong wedge for this firm.
- "I don't trust AI with client data" → trust-and-security gap.

Each of these is more valuable than a soft "let me think about it" yes.

## SECTION 8 — THE SCORING RUBRIC

### 8.1 The 5-axis matrix

For each interview × each of 5 verbs = 150 scored cells. Each cell is rated 1-5 on each axis.

| Axis | 1 | 3 | 5 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Severity** | Indifferent; doesn't come up unprompted | Annoyed; complains when asked | Visceral; spontaneous 4-min story with named client and dollar loss |
| **Frequency** | Annually or rarer | Monthly | Weekly or more |
| **Workaround quality** | Perfect — clean tool handles it | Partial workaround — Excel macro, manual review | None — partner says "we just suffer" |
| **WTP** | < ₹1,000 / month or per cycle | ₹2,000-10,000 / month or ₹10K-50K / cycle | > ₹25,000 / month sustained or > ₹1L / cycle |
| **Buyer authority** | Committee decision, multiple partners must agree | Partner decides with input | Sole partner decision; same person who feels pain owns the budget |

### 8.2 Scoring conventions

- Score from **behavioral evidence only**. If the partner says "I'd pay ₹10K/month" but the WTP probe shows current tooling spend < ₹2K/month → score WTP as 1, not 5.
- If a verb wasn't probed (partner ran short on time, or "we don't do that"), mark as N/A. Do not impute zeros.
- Score within 6 hours of the interview. Day-late scoring drifts upward due to recency bias.

### 8.3 Aggregation across 30 interviews

For each verb, compute:
- **Wedge score** = Severity_avg + Frequency_avg + (6 − Workaround_avg) + WTP_avg + Buyer_authority_avg. (Inverted workaround so higher = more painful.) Max = 25 per verb.
- **Top wedges** = top 3 verbs by Wedge score across 30 interviews.

### 8.4 Decision thresholds

| Outcome | Threshold | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Strong wedge** | One verb scores ≥ 18 average across 30 interviews | Commit. Build first deliverable. |
| **Plausible wedge** | One verb scores 15-17 average; 5+ partners qualified as design partners on it | Build a wireframe / spec; do 5 deeper deliverable interviews on that verb before committing. |
| **Weak signal** | All 5 verbs score < 15 average | Wedge thesis is wrong. Either (a) the 5 verbs are not the right verbs, or (b) Indian CA firms are not the right ICP. Pause; redesign 10 more interviews around adjacent verbs (TDS, MCA, ROC, transfer pricing, internal audit) before committing. |
| **Two verbs tied at top** | Top two verbs within 1.5 points of each other | The wedge is the **intersection** — figure out which workflow has both as adjacent steps and build the combined slice. |

### 8.5 Pivot signal

If after 30 interviews:
- No verb scores > 15, AND
- Fewer than 3 design partners qualify (per Section 5.5)

…then the **ICP is wrong**, not the verb. Possible pivots: Indian SMBs direct (rather than CA firms), Indian Big-4 GDS captives, offshore-to-US shops, GCCs. Each is a separate research effort.

## SECTION 9 — APPENDICES

### 9.1 Pre-interview prep checklist (5 items)

Complete each item the morning of the interview:

1. **LinkedIn skim (10 min):** Partner's role, years in practice, firm size estimate from connection count, any media appearances. Note 1 personal hook (alumni network, hometown, specialization).
2. **Firm website scan (5 min):** Service line page — are they audit-heavy, tax-heavy, advisory-heavy, GST-specialist? This biases which of the 5 verbs is in scope.
3. **ICAI member directory check (3 min):** Validate firm registration; check FRN; note if firm has multiple partners (consensus-buyer risk).
4. **Recent ICAI / regulatory news (5 min):** Is there a current notice or deadline in the news that could anchor the conversation? (e.g., recent GSTR-3B hard-locking circular, ITR utility update, MCA portal outage.)
5. **The warm intro context (5 min):** Who introduced them? What did the introducer say? Re-read the intro thread. Open with: "Rohit said you're the person to talk to about [X]" — name the introducer in the first 30 seconds.

### 9.3 The "Tell me you don't know 3CD" disarm script

The founder has no accounting background. He has two choices: hide it (lose credibility when caught) or surface it (disarm and accelerate trust). The Mom Test endorses option 2.

**Use this script when the partner uses jargon the founder doesn't know:**

> "Wait — sorry. Treat me like a complete beginner here. I'm a tech guy, I have a warm CA network but I don't have a CA background. So when you say [term], can you explain what that actually means in your day-to-day? I'd rather waste 30 seconds now than miss the whole point."

**Why this works:**
- It reverses the power dynamic in the partner's favor. CAs respond to teaching — it's their professional self-image.
- It forces concrete examples (you can't explain "Clause 21 of 3CD" abstractly to a layperson; you have to use a real client).
- It immunizes the rest of the interview from bluffing. The partner will now over-explain — pure signal.

**Use this script at the START if the founder feels the partner is being defensive:**

> "Look, I'll be honest — I'm not a CA. I'm here to learn, not to pitch. My only goal in this hour is to understand what your week actually looks like, well enough that I don't sound like an idiot when I talk to your peers. Can you bear with me?"

This works in 90%+ of cases. CAs receive 3-5 vendor cold-calls a week; the founder differentiating as "the curious non-CA" is high-leverage positioning.

**Use this script at MID-INTERVIEW when energy flags:**

> "Sorry, can you pause for a second? You mentioned [X] and [Y] — are those the same thing or different? I want to make sure I'm not confusing two different problems in my head."

### 9.4 The 3-question warm-intro qualifier

Before scheduling 30 interviews, the founder will get 40-60 warm intros. Not all are equal. Use these 3 questions over email/WhatsApp before booking:

**Q1 (firm size + role):**
> "Quick context to make our chat useful — roughly how many people are at your firm including articles? And are you the managing partner / one of the partners / a senior CA in practice?"

**Q2 (service-line filter):**
> "Of your client work, would you say it's mostly statutory audit + tax audit, mostly GST + TDS + ITR compliance, or mostly advisory / consulting? Rough %?"

**Q3 (recency probe):**
> "Have you tried any new software or workflow tool in the last 6 months? I'm only asking to make sure I don't waste your time covering ground you already know."

**Scoring rule:** Schedule the interview only if:
- The partner is a partner or senior practitioner (not an article or paid staff member). [Q1]
- The firm does either audit OR GST-heavy compliance (covers 4 of 5 verbs). [Q2]
- The partner has tried OR rejected a new tool recently (signals active evaluation muscle). [Q3]

A partner who answers all three quickly is also signaling responsiveness — the strongest behavioral pre-screen. If they take >7 days to answer 3 short questions, the interview itself will be flaky.

### 9.5 The 6-week interview plan

**Week 1 (interviews 1-5):**
- 5 Tier-1 small firms (lowest scheduling friction; warmest intros).
- Goal: calibrate the script. Discard the 5-verb scoring on these; treat as instrument calibration.
- Refine the kit based on what didn't work. Lock the script by end of Week 1.

**Week 2 (interviews 6-12):**
- 4 Tier-1 partners, 3 Tier-1.5 partners.
- Mix of solo (2), small (4), mid-tier (1).
- This is the first **real scoring batch**. By end of Week 2, founder should have an emerging top-2 verb hypothesis.

**Week 3 (interviews 13-18):**
- 6 Tier-1.5 partners. Mix of solo (2), small (3), mid-tier (1).
- Use the emerging hypothesis to ask **sharper** follow-ups on the top-2 verbs. Don't pitch; deepen.
- First-deliverable test offered to 3-4 strongest design partners.

**Week 4 (interviews 19-24):**
- 6 Tier-2 partners. Mix of solo (3), small (3), mid-tier (0).
- Tier-2 is the contrarian sample — different economics, different software exposure. If the wedge survives Tier-2, it's robust.
- First-deliverable test offered to 2-3 more candidates.

**Week 5 (interviews 25-30):**
- 6 partners chosen to fill matrix gaps. E.g., if mid-tier under-represented, do 3 mid-tier. If notice-reply verb under-probed, target practitioners with appellate practice.
- Final 6 are confirmation, not discovery. Founder should already have a thesis by Week 5.

**Week 6 (synthesis):**
- No new interviews.
- Day 1-2: Score all 30 interviews in the matrix.
- Day 3: Aggregate; identify top wedge.
- Day 4: Re-contact 4-7 design partners; commit to first-deliverable cohort.
- Day 5: Write a 3-page wedge memo; share with 2-3 trusted advisors (NOT with CAs in the interview pool, who would feel pitched).

**Buffer rules:**
- Expect 30% no-show / reschedule rate. Schedule 40 interviews to get 30 done.
- Front-load Mondays / Tuesdays. CAs are most responsive early-week when not in compliance crunch.
- Avoid scheduling interviews 5-25 of every month (peak GST cycle). Best windows: 1st-4th and 25th-end of month.
- Avoid Sept entirely (3CD crunch); avoid second half of July (ITR crunch); de-emphasize April (year-end audit).
